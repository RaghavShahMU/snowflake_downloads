Prompts classification readout

Last updated: 2026-04-03

What this is: How wording patterns in agent prompts line up with post-launch success for ~115K classified agents (same success rules as the main readout).

Field guide: Dimension labels like functional_archetype_creator mean “creator style job” — hover dotted terms for definitions and counts, or see the classification schema.

Executive summary

  • Headline: Agents described as working on a bounded list or queue (“collection-scoped”) and agents framed as monitors / observers are far more likely to look like successful cohort members than agents described as handling one-off user chats or pure content-creation roles.
  • Pattern, not prescription: These are statistical links in our data, not guarantees — useful for prioritizing templates, onboarding, and product positioning.
  • What’s below: Two expanded examples (execution + archetype), then optional detail — correlation heatmaps, a technical model chart, and methods for analysts.

Key findings

Finding 1 — How the work is scoped — List/queue-style scope lines up with success; one-off user-message scope does not.
  • collection_scoped — strongest lift in our models (random forest + logistic).
  • single_user_prompt / single_asset_from_user — among the strongest drags.
  • collection_unbounded — also positive; automation-on-event patterns are mixed.

Highlighted correlations

The strongest prompt×tool pairs are often label–instrument matches (e.g. “structured document” prompts lining up with document tools, or queue-scoped work lining up with task filters). Those are useful for sanity-checking the classifiers, but they are not strategically surprising. The patterns below cut across that layer: they sit where job framing and tooling pull in different directions, in line with who actually succeeds in the cohort.

  • Creator archetype vs. queue mechanics. functional_archetype_creator shows one of the strongest negative ties to todo_write (r ≈ −0.40) and to retrieve_tasks_by_filters (r ≈ −0.36). Creator-heavy prompts cluster on conversational and generative tools; they largely step away from the list-and-filter primitives that define the highest-performing execution patterns. That is a product-relevant gap: the most common “job story” is not wired into the workflows that already work.
  • Outbound data flow vs. task hygiene. data_flow_direction_outbound lines up with weaker use of todo_write (r ≈ −0.35) and retrieve_tasks_by_filters (r ≈ −0.30). “Ships net-new work out of ClickUp” framing anti-aligns with checklist mechanics beyond archetype alone — a broad producer vs. operator split worth naming in templates and onboarding.
  • Single-user-prompt scope vs. structured tracking. execution_dataset_single_user_prompt negatively tracks todo_write (r ≈ −0.33). Ad-hoc “answer this message” semantics drift away from durable work tracking, which matches the success model (collection-scoped patterns win; one-off prompt patterns struggle).
  • ClickUp-only vs. web stack. external_integration_scope_clickup_only lines up with much lower use of search_public_web (r ≈ −0.31) and load_web_pages (r ≈ −0.28). Directionally obvious, but the magnitude underscores how tightly integration scope and real tool mix move together — siloed prompts and siloed capabilities show up as one object.
  • Consultative autonomy vs. checklist tools. autonomy_level_consultative negatively tracks todo_write (r ≈ −0.28). Human-gated flows are less entangled with todo/list primitives; whether that is healthy delegation or under-automation depends on use case, but it is a consistent behavioral signature.
